Harriet Tubman

American abolitionist and political activist

∼ A brief historical timeline of Harriet Tubman's life ∽

  • March 1822 - Born enslaved as Araminta Ross in Dorchester County, Maryland, U.S.
  • As an adolescent - Suffered a severe head injury when her overseer threw a metal weight at her head. This caused Ross (Tubman) to suffer from painful headaches and seizures for the remainder of her life.
  • Around 1844 - Married a free Black man named John Tubman. Araminta changed her name to Harriet shortly after her marriage
  • September 17, 1849 - Tubman and her brothers escaped slavery from the Poplar Neck Plantation in Caroline County. Shortly after, Tubman's brothers had second thoughts and forced her to return to the plantation with them.
  • 1850 - U.S. Congress passed the Fugitive Slave Act. This law heavily punished abetting escape and increased the risks for escaped slaves.
  • Soon afterward - She again escaped slavery, this time using the Underground Railroad to make her way nearly 90 miles through Delaware and Pennsylvania.
  • April 1858 - Met the abolitionist and insurgent, John Brown. Tubman then helped Brown recruit supporters for the raid on Harper's Ferry.
  • Early 1859 - She purchased a small piece of land outside of Auburn, New York from U.S. Senator William Seward. This land became a safe haven for many of Tubman's friends and family.
  • June 2, 1863 - During the Combahee River Raid, she helped rescue more than 750 slaves. Newspapers at the time spoke of Tubman's "patriotism, sagacity, energy, and ability".
  • March 18, 1869 - She married Nelson Charles Davis at the Central Presbyterian Church in Auburn, New York.
  • In her later years - Tubman became heavily involved in the woman's suffrage movement. She spoke in New York, Boston, and Washington D.C. on the importance of a woman's right to vote.
  • 1913 - Tubman passed away of pneumonia while living in the Harriet Tubman Home for the Aged.

You can find more detailed information on the life of Harriet Tubman on her Wikipedia page and at Harriet-Tubman.org/timeline/.
